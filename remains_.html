5Ws

Who?

Who is speaking, and what experience is he remembering?

The speaker is a British soldier recalling a shooting during service in Iraq and the guilt that follows him afterwards.

What?

What happens in the poem, and what continues after the event?

Soldiers kill a looter during patrol. Afterwards, the speaker is haunted by trauma, guilt, flashbacks and uncertainty.

Where?

Where does the event happen, and where does the memory continue?

The shooting happens during modern conflict in Iraq, but the memory follows the speaker home and remains in his mind.

When?

When is the poem set, and how does time shift?

The poem moves between the moment of the shooting and the speaker’s later memories, showing that trauma continues after war.

Why?

Why does Armitage present this memory so directly?

Armitage exposes the moral ambiguity of war and shows that soldiers can remain psychologically damaged long after violence ends.
REMAINS — Simon Armitage
On another occasion, we got sent out to tackle looters raiding a bank. And one of them legs it up the road, probably armed, possibly not. Well myself and somebody else and somebody else are all of the same mind, so all three of us open fire. Three of a kind all letting fly, and I swear I see every round as it rips through his life – I see broad daylight on the other side. So we’ve hit this looter a dozen times and he’s there on the ground, sort of inside out, pain itself, the image of agony. One of my mates goes by and tosses his guts back into his body. Then he’s carted off in the back of a lorry. End of story, except not really. His blood-shadow stays on the street, and out on patrol I walk right over it week after week. Then I’m home on leave. But I blink and he bursts again through the doors of the bank. Sleep, and he’s probably armed, and possibly not. Dream, and he’s torn apart by a dozen rounds. And the drink and the drugs won’t flush him out – he’s here in my head when I close my eyes, dug in behind enemy lines, not left for dead in some distant, sun-stunned, sand-smothered land or six-feetunder in desert sand, but near to the knuckle, here and now, his bloody life in my bloody hands.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“probably armed, possibly not.”

Look at the uncertainty in the two modal words.
  • “Probably” suggests the speaker wants to believe the shooting was justified.
  • “Possibly” leaves open the chance that the man was innocent.
  • The uncertainty creates guilt that returns later in the poem.
QUOTE 2

“tosses his guts back into his body.”

Focus on the casual verb and the graphic body image.
  • “Tosses” sounds casual, suggesting desensitisation.
  • “Guts” creates graphic, shocking imagery.
  • Armitage strips away any glamour from war.
QUOTE 3

“his bloody life in my bloody hands.”

Think about repetition and the idiom “blood on my hands”.
  • “Bloody” suggests literal blood and moral blame.
  • “My” makes the guilt personal and individual.
  • The final line shows guilt remains after the battlefield.

Context

Hint: think about modern warfare, PTSD, and why Armitage uses a soldier’s speaking voice.
  • Simon Armitage wrote Remains after interviewing soldiers who served in Iraq and Afghanistan.
  • The poem is linked to The Not Dead, a project exploring soldiers’ experiences after war.
  • It focuses on PTSD, guilt and the psychological effects of combat.
  • Armitage challenges romantic ideas about war by presenting its disturbing reality.

Remains – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Armitage present guilt in the poem, Remains?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on guilt and trauma.
  • Introduces the poem’s central idea that war continues after the event.
  • Establishes Armitage’s wider message about the lasting effects of violent choices.
In Remains, Simon Armitage presents guilt as immediate, disturbing and impossible to escape. The poem follows a soldier who remembers shooting a looter during war, but the real conflict begins afterwards when the memory keeps returning. Through conversational voice, violent imagery and structural shifts, Armitage suggests that guilt can stay trapped in the mind long after the physical event has ended.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how guilt begins with uncertainty.
  • Uses the quotation “probably armed, possibly not.”
  • Zooms in on the modal word “possibly.”
  • Explores repetition and moral ambiguity.
  • Links to the realities of modern warfare and split-second decisions.
Armitage first presents guilt through uncertainty about whether the shooting was justified. This is clear in the repeated line “probably armed, possibly not.” The modal adverb “possibly” is unsettling because it leaves open the chance that the man was innocent. Although the speaker tries to reassure himself with “probably,” the second phrase undermines that confidence and becomes the seed of his guilt. The repetition later in the poem shows that this doubt keeps replaying in his mind. Contextually, Armitage wrote the poem to reflect the moral dilemmas faced by soldiers in Iraq, suggesting that one uncertain decision in war can produce lasting psychological damage.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the brutality of the memory.
  • Uses the shocking image as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the verb “tosses.”
  • Explores desensitisation and dehumanisation.
  • Links to Armitage’s purpose of exposing war’s reality.
Armitage also presents guilt by showing how horrifying the event remains in the speaker’s memory. This is evident when one soldier “tosses his guts back into his body.” The verb “tosses” sounds casual and careless, which makes the image even more shocking. It suggests the men have become desensitised to violence, while the victim is reduced to a damaged object rather than a person. However, the speaker cannot stay detached, because the grotesque detail is remembered so vividly. Armitage uses this graphic language to strip away any glamorous idea of war and instead reveal its brutality. The effect is that guilt grows not only from the killing itself, but from the way conflict destroys normal human feeling and respect for life.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how guilt becomes personal and permanent.
  • Uses the final line “his bloody life in my bloody hands.”
  • Zooms in on the repeated word “bloody.”
  • Explores the ending and the isolated final couplet.
  • Links to PTSD and the lasting consequences of war.
By the end of the poem, guilt has become completely personal and inescapable. This is shown in the final line “his bloody life in my bloody hands.” The repetition of “bloody” emphasises both literal violence and moral blame, echoing the idea of having blood on your hands. Earlier the poem uses collective pronouns such as “we,” but here the guilt belongs only to “my” hands, showing how responsibility has narrowed onto the speaker alone. Structurally, the isolated final couplet gives this confession extra force and makes the poem end on guilt rather than action. Armitage suggests that, for many soldiers, the war does not remain overseas: it continues internally through trauma, memory and the pressure of living with what they have done.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Armitage’s presentation of guilt.
  • Returns to the contrast between physical war and mental suffering.
  • Highlights the poem’s warning about the lasting cost of conflict.
In conclusion, Armitage presents guilt in Remains as a lasting wound that survives long after the violence is over. The soldier may have left the battlefield, but he cannot escape the memory or the uncertainty surrounding what happened. Through repetition, disturbing imagery and a confessional structure, the poem shows that the deepest damage of war can remain in the mind.
