I SHALL RETURN — Claude McKay
I shall return again; I shall return To laugh and love and watch with wonder-eyes At golden noon the forest fires burn, Wafting their blue-black smoke to sapphire skies. I shall return to loiter by the streams That bathe the brown blades of the bending grasses, And realise once more my thousand dreams Of waters rushing down the mountain passes. I shall return to hear the fiddle and fife Of village dances, dear delicious tunes That stir the hidden depths of native life, Stray melodies of dim remembered runes. I shall return, I shall return again, To ease my mind of long, long years of pain.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“I shall return again; I shall return”

Think about repetition and certainty.
  • “Shall” sounds definite and determined.
  • The repeated phrase creates a refrain, showing the thought dominates his mind.
  • Home is presented as emotionally necessary, not just remembered.
QUOTE 2

“And realise once more my thousand dreams / Of waters rushing down the mountain passes”

Ask what “thousand dreams” suggests about memory.
  • “Thousand dreams” implies repeated, idealised memories of home.
  • The rushing water makes the remembered landscape vivid and alive.
  • McKay suggests memory sustains the speaker while he is away.
QUOTE 3

“To ease my mind of long, long years of pain”

Think about why the poem ends with pain.
  • The repetition of “long” stresses prolonged suffering.
  • “Ease” presents home as a source of healing.
  • The final couplet reveals the emotional cost of exile.

Context

Make notes first, then reveal.

Hint: think about McKay’s migration, the Harlem Renaissance, and the emotional meaning of Jamaica.
  • Claude McKay was born in rural Jamaica in 1889 and later migrated to America.
  • He became a key figure in the Harlem Renaissance, celebrating Black art, identity and culture.
  • I Shall Return was published in Harlem Shadows in 1922.
  • The poem is at least partly autobiographical: McKay longed for Jamaica but never returned there.

Structure

Add structure notes, then reveal.

Hint: look at the sonnet form, repeated title phrase, and final couplet.
  • The poem is a Shakespearean sonnet, linking love poetry to love for homeland.
  • The repeated refrain “I shall return” reinforces longing and certainty.
  • The first two quatrains focus on natural beauty; the third shifts to music, dancing and community.
  • The final couplet reveals that separation from home has caused “long, long years of pain.”

I Shall Return – Question 1 Model

Question: How does McKay present longing for home in I Shall Return?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on longing, memory and belonging.
  • Introduces the idea that home is both a physical place and an emotional need.
  • Establishes McKay’s wider message about exile and the power of memory.
In I Shall Return, Claude McKay presents longing for home as powerful, deeply emotional and impossible to ignore. The speaker imagines returning to his homeland and describes it through vivid natural and cultural imagery, showing how strongly he still feels connected to it. Through repetition, sensory detail and the sonnet form, McKay suggests that home is not simply a place the speaker remembers, but something that offers comfort, identity and healing after years of separation.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the speaker’s certainty about returning.
  • Uses the repeated declaration “I shall return again.”
  • Zooms in on the modal verb “shall.”
  • Explores the effect of repetition across the sonnet.
  • Links to McKay’s migration and longing for Jamaica.
McKay first presents longing for home as certain and all-consuming through the repeated declaration “I shall return again.” The modal verb “shall” sounds definite and determined, suggesting that the speaker’s desire to go back is not a vague hope but something he feels must happen. The repetition of this phrase throughout the poem gives it the force of a refrain, showing that the idea of return dominates his thoughts. This is significant because McKay had left rural Jamaica and lived abroad, so the poem can be read as at least partly autobiographical. By opening with such certainty, McKay shows that the speaker’s connection to home has remained strong despite distance and time.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the speaker’s idealised memories of home.
  • Uses the quotation about “my thousand dreams.”
  • Zooms in on the hyperbole “thousand.”
  • Explores vivid natural imagery and precious colours.
  • Links to Harlem Renaissance ideas of identity and heritage.
McKay also presents longing for home through idealised and repeated memories. This is clear in the line “And realise once more my thousand dreams.” The hyperbolic word “thousand” suggests that the speaker has imagined home over and over again, revealing how often he escapes into memory. Around this quotation, McKay fills the poem with rich natural imagery such as “golden noon” and “sapphire skies,” which make the homeland seem beautiful and precious. The first two quatrains focus closely on streams, grasses and mountain passes, so the landscape feels vivid and alive in the speaker’s mind. As a poet associated with the Harlem Renaissance, McKay often explored Black identity and cultural belonging, and here home is presented as a source of pride and emotional rootedness.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that home is presented as a source of healing.
  • Uses the final line about “long, long years of pain.”
  • Zooms in on the repetition of “long.”
  • Explores the significance of the closing couplet in the sonnet.
  • Links to exile and the emotional cost of separation.
By the end of the poem, McKay shows that the speaker’s longing for home is rooted in pain as well as love. In the final line, he says he wants to return “To ease my mind of long, long years of pain.” The repetition of “long” emphasises the duration of his suffering and suggests that separation from home has deeply affected him emotionally. This is especially powerful because it appears in the closing couplet of a Shakespearean sonnet, where the poem’s final truth is often revealed. Earlier images of music, dancing and natural beauty now seem even more important because they are not just pleasant memories: they are the things that could heal him. McKay therefore presents home as essential to the speaker’s sense of peace, identity and emotional survival.

Conclusion

  • Summarises McKay’s presentation of longing for home.
  • Returns to the contrast between beautiful memory and painful exile.
  • Highlights the poem’s message that home shapes identity and emotional wellbeing.
In conclusion, McKay presents longing for home as intense, lasting and deeply personal. Although the speaker remembers his homeland as beautiful and full of life, the poem also makes clear that exile has caused real pain. Through repetition, sensory imagery and sonnet form, McKay shows that home can remain central to a person’s identity even when they are physically separated from it.
