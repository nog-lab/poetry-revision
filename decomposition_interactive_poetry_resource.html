5Ws

Who?

Who is the speaker observing, and what is his social position?

The speaker observes an old beggar sleeping on a pavement in Bombay: vulnerable, poor and ignored by society.

What?

What has the speaker done, and why does he later feel guilty?

He has taken a photograph and treated the man’s suffering as a “composition”, then regrets exploiting hunger and solitude as art.

Where?

Where is the poem set, and why does this setting matter?

It is set in Bombay, now Mumbai, where public poverty and inequality make the beggar’s suffering seem disturbingly “common”.

When?

When was the poem written, and what wider historical background matters?

Written in 1967, it reflects post-colonial India and Ghose’s awareness of migration, poverty, homelessness and social division.

Why?

Why might Ghose want readers to feel uncomfortable?

Ghose criticises indifference and asks whether it is morally wrong to turn another person’s pain into art.
DECOMPOSITION — Zulfikar Ghose
I have a picture I took in Bombay of a beggar asleep on the pavement: grey-haired, wearing shorts and a dirty shirt, his shadow thrown aside like a blanket. His arms and legs could be cracks in the stone, routes for the ants' journeys, the flies' descents, Brain-washed by the sun into exhaustion, he lies veined into stone, a fossil man. Behind him there is a crowd passingly bemused by a pavement trickster and quite indifferent to this very common sight of an old man asleep on the pavement. I thought it then a good composition and glibly called it "The Man in the Street," remarking how typical it was of India that the man in the street lived there. His head in the posture of one weeping into a pillow chides me now for my presumption at attempting to compose art of his hunger and solitude.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“a beggar asleep on the pavement”

Think about the label “beggar” and the uncomfortable place where he sleeps.
  • “Beggar” reduces him to poverty rather than identity.
  • “Asleep” suggests vulnerability and defencelessness.
  • “Pavement” makes his suffering public, harsh and exposed.
QUOTE 2

“he lies veined into stone, a fossil man”

Look closely at the metaphor and how it turns the man into part of the street.
  • “Veined” merges the man’s body with the pavement.
  • “Stone” suggests hardness, lifelessness and neglect.
  • “Fossil man” dehumanises him, making him seem like a remnant rather than a living person.
QUOTE 3

“quite / indifferent to this very common sight”

Focus on the crowd’s reaction and what “common” suggests about repeated suffering.
  • “Indifferent” suggests emotional coldness and a lack of compassion.
  • “Very common” implies poverty has become normalised.
  • The crowd’s apathy makes society seem morally careless.

Hint: think about Bombay, poverty, Ghose’s migration, and the ethics of turning suffering into art.

  • The poem is set in Bombay, now Mumbai, a crowded city associated here with poverty and public inequality.
  • Ghose’s work often explores poverty, helplessness, death, decay and selfishness.
  • The poem questions whether it is ethical to use another person’s suffering as artistic material.

Decomposition – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Ghose present human suffering in the poem, Decomposition?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on suffering and dehumanisation.
  • Introduces the poem’s criticism of social indifference and the speaker’s guilt.
  • Establishes Ghose’s wider message about the ethics of turning pain into art.
In Decomposition, Zulfikar Ghose presents human suffering as physical, degrading and disturbingly easy for society to ignore. The poem describes a beggar sleeping on a pavement in Bombay, but it also becomes a reflection on the speaker’s own failure to respond with compassion. Through harsh imagery, metaphor and a structural shift from observation to self-criticism, Ghose suggests that suffering does not only damage the victim: it also exposes the cruelty and moral carelessness of those who look on.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how the beggar’s suffering is shown as dehumanising.
  • Uses the opening image of the “beggar asleep on the pavement.”
  • Zooms in on the label “beggar” and the setting of the pavement.
  • Explores how the simile and physical detail make him seem abandoned.
  • Links to the poem’s setting in Bombay and wider poverty.
Ghose first presents human suffering as something that strips a person of dignity and identity. This is clear in the description of “a beggar asleep on the pavement.” The noun “beggar” reduces the man to his poverty rather than recognising him as an individual, while the harsh image of the “pavement” suggests discomfort, exposure and neglect. Ghose develops this further when the man’s “shadow” is described as being thrown aside “like a blanket,” a simile which hints at how little protection he has. In the context of Bombay, a crowded city marked by visible inequality, the man’s suffering is presented as part of an urban reality that has become normalised. Ghose wants the reader to feel uneasy about how easily such a life can be overlooked.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies how suffering makes the man seem almost non-human.
  • Uses the metaphor of the “fossil man.”
  • Zooms in on “stone” and “fossil.”
  • Explores how the body is merged with the street.
  • Links to Ghose’s interest in decay, helplessness and marginalisation.
Ghose also presents suffering as so extreme that it turns the beggar into something lifeless and object-like. This is shown in the metaphor “he lies veined into stone, a fossil man.” The image of being “veined into stone” suggests that his body has become part of the pavement itself, making him seem trapped and almost erased. The phrase “fossil man” is especially powerful because a fossil is a preserved remnant from the past, not a living person with a future. This dehumanises the beggar and shows how long-term poverty can make someone seem invisible to the world around them. Ghose’s focus on decay reflects his wider concern with helplessness and social neglect, and the reader is forced to confront the shocking way in which suffering can make a human being appear less than human.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that the poem also criticises the response to suffering.
  • Uses the phrase “indifferent to this very common sight.”
  • Zooms in on “indifferent” and “common.”
  • Explores the speaker’s guilty reflection at the end of the poem.
  • Links structure to the shift from description to moral self-judgement.
Most importantly, Ghose presents human suffering as something society has become numb to. The crowd are described as “indifferent to this very common sight,” and the adjective “indifferent” suggests emotional coldness and a complete lack of compassion. The phrase “very common” is equally disturbing because it implies that repeated exposure to poverty has made people stop noticing it. However, the poem does not only criticise the crowd: it also condemns the speaker, who admits he “glibly” turned the image into art before later regretting his “presumption.” Structurally, the ending shifts from detached description to personal guilt, showing that the speaker now understands the beggar’s suffering should never have been treated as a mere “composition.” Ghose therefore suggests that the real horror lies not only in poverty itself, but in the selfishness of those who observe it without acting.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Ghose’s presentation of suffering.
  • Returns to the contrast between real pain and public indifference.
  • Highlights the poem’s warning about dehumanisation and moral failure.
In conclusion, Ghose presents human suffering in Decomposition as brutal, dehumanising and morally challenging. The beggar’s condition shows the physical effects of poverty, while the reactions of the crowd and the speaker reveal how easily suffering can be ignored or exploited. Through vivid imagery, metaphor and reflective structure, the poem warns readers against becoming indifferent to the pain of others.
