Cousin Kate

5Ws

Write answers on your printed copy first. Reveal each prompt only to check or improve your notes.

Who?

Who is speaking, and who is she addressing?

A working-class woman speaks directly to her cousin Kate, accusing her and the lord who seduced then abandoned her.

What?

What has happened to the speaker?

A powerful lord has used her, rejected her, married Kate, and left the speaker socially shamed.

Where?

Where is the poem set, and why does class matter?

It is set in a rural Victorian world of cottages, estates and strict class divisions, where the lord has far more power than the women.

When?

When was the poem written, and what social attitudes shape it?

Written in 1862, during the Victorian period, when women were judged harshly for sexual relationships outside marriage.

Why?

Why does Rossetti present this story through the speaker’s voice?

Rossetti exposes gender double standards and gives a shamed, marginalised woman a voice of anger, dignity and defiance.
COUSIN KATE — Christina Rossetti
I was a cottage maiden Hardened by sun and air, Contented with my cottage mates, Not mindful I was fair. Why did a great lord find me out, And praise my flaxen hair? Why did a great lord find me out, To fill my heart with care? He lured me to his palace home— Woe’s me for joy thereof— To lead a shameless shameful life, His plaything and his love. He wore me like a silken knot, He changed me like a glove; So now I moan, an unclean thing, Who might have been a dove. O Lady Kate, my cousin Kate, You grew more fair than I: He saw you at your father’s gate, Chose you, and cast me by. He watched your steps along the lane, Your work among the rye; He lifted you from mean estate To sit with him on high.
Because you were so good and pure He bound you with his ring: The neighbours call you good and pure, Call me an outcast thing. Even so I sit and howl in dust, You sit in gold and sing: Now which of us has tenderer heart? You had the stronger wing. O cousin Kate, my love was true, Your love was writ in sand: If he had fooled not me but you, If you stood where I stand, He’d not have won me with his love Nor bought me with his land; I would have spit into his face And not have taken his hand. Yet I’ve a gift you have not got, And seem not like to get: For all your clothes and wedding-ring I’ve little doubt you fret. My fair-haired son, my shame, my pride, Cling closer, closer yet: Your father would give lands for one To wear his coronet.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“I was a cottage maiden / Hardened by sun and air”

Look at how Rossetti presents the speaker before the lord exploits her.
  • “Maiden” suggests innocence and purity.
  • “Cottage” shows her lower class and simple rural life.
  • Rossetti makes her exploitation seem more unjust.
QUOTE 2

“Call me an outcast thing”

Ask what society has done to her identity and status.
  • “Outcast” shows she is rejected by society.
  • “Thing” dehumanises her and strips away identity.
  • Rossetti criticises the unfair judgement of women.
QUOTE 3

“My fair-haired son, my shame, my pride”

Think about the contrast between disgrace and triumph.
  • “Shame” reflects society’s judgement of unmarried motherhood.
  • “Pride” shows defiance and personal love.
  • The son gives her power because he could be the lord’s heir.

Context

Make notes first, then reveal.

Hint: think about Victorian expectations of women, class, purity and marriage.
  • Written by Christina Rossetti, a Victorian poet interested in love, morality and women’s experience.
  • Victorian society expected women to be pure, obedient and respectable.
  • Women were often blamed for sexual behaviour while powerful men escaped punishment.
  • The poem criticises a patriarchal culture where class, marriage and heirs decide female worth.

Structure

Add structure notes, then reveal.

Hint: look at the speaker’s voice, the ballad-like storytelling and the final shift in power.
  • The poem is a dramatic monologue, giving the rejected woman a direct voice.
  • It has a ballad-like form, telling a moral story about love, betrayal and social judgement.
  • Six regular stanzas create control, contrasting with the speaker’s anger and pain.
  • The final stanza shifts towards defiance as the speaker reveals the son Kate cannot give the lord.

Cousin Kate – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Rossetti present gender inequality in Cousin Kate?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on double standards.
  • Introduces the contrast between the speaker’s shame and the lord’s power.
  • Establishes Rossetti’s criticism of a society that judges women unfairly.
In Cousin Kate, Christina Rossetti presents gender inequality as cruel, unfair and deeply rooted in Victorian society. The speaker is used and abandoned by a wealthy lord, yet it is she who is blamed and shamed, while he keeps his power and status. Through the dramatic monologue, Rossetti gives a marginalised woman a strong voice and exposes the double standards that judged women by purity, marriage and motherhood.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the speaker’s innocence and lower status.
  • Uses the quotation about the “cottage maiden.”
  • Zooms in on the noun “maiden.”
  • Explores the contrast between rural simplicity and male power.
  • Links to Victorian expectations of female purity and class.
At the start of the poem, Rossetti presents the speaker as innocent and vulnerable, which makes the lord’s treatment of her seem even more unjust. This is shown in the line “I was a cottage maiden / Hardened by sun and air.” The noun “maiden” suggests innocence and purity, showing that she begins as someone society should respect. However, she is also clearly working-class, and this makes her easier for the lord to exploit. Rossetti creates a sharp contrast between the speaker’s simple rural life and the power of the “great lord,” suggesting that gender inequality is made even worse by class inequality. In Victorian society, women were expected to remain pure and obedient, but powerful men were often excused for behaviour that ruined women’s lives.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies society’s unfair judgement of women.
  • Uses the phrase “outcast thing” as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the noun “thing.”
  • Explores how the speaker is dehumanised by others.
  • Links to Rossetti’s criticism of patriarchal values.
Rossetti also presents gender inequality through the way society judges the speaker after the affair. This is clear when the speaker says the neighbours “Call me an outcast thing.” The noun “thing” is dehumanising and suggests that she has lost her identity and worth in the eyes of society. The word “outcast” shows she has been pushed outside normal social life, even though the lord is equally responsible. Rossetti’s choice to use a dramatic monologue is important because it allows the speaker to expose this injustice directly. The repeated contrast between Kate being called “good and pure” and the speaker being rejected highlights the hypocrisy of a patriarchal society where women are judged by sexual reputation while men remain powerful and unpunished.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how the ending challenges male power.
  • Uses the quotation “my shame, my pride.”
  • Zooms in on the contrast between “shame” and “pride.”
  • Explores the speaker’s final defiance and triumph.
  • Links to the importance of heirs in Victorian society.
By the end of the poem, Rossetti shows that although society has shamed the speaker, she still finds a form of power through motherhood. This is shown in the line “My fair-haired son, my shame, my pride.” The contrast between “shame” and “pride” reveals her complicated position: society sees the child as disgraceful, but she sees him as precious. This suggests that her personal values are now stronger than society’s judgement. The final stanza shifts the balance of power because Kate has status, wealth and a wedding ring, yet the speaker has the son the lord desires as an heir. In a Victorian world where family name and inheritance mattered greatly, this ending becomes a kind of revenge. Rossetti therefore suggests that although women are oppressed by inequality, they can still resist it and challenge the values that try to silence them.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Rossetti’s presentation of gender inequality.
  • Returns to the contrast between male power and female shame.
  • Highlights the poem’s criticism of Victorian double standards.
In conclusion, Rossetti presents gender inequality in Cousin Kate as deeply unfair and damaging. The speaker is blamed, rejected and humiliated, while the lord keeps his social power. Through contrast, voice and irony, Rossetti criticises a society that values women only through purity and marriage, while also allowing the speaker a final moment of dignity and defiance.
