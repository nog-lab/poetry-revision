DUSTING THE PHONE — Jackie Kay
I am spending my time imagining the worst that could happen. I know this is not a good idea, and that being in love, I could be spending my time going over the best that has been happening. The phone rings heralding some disaster. Sirens. Or it doesn’t ring which also means disaster. Sirens. In which case, who would ring me to tell? Nobody knows. The future is a long gloved hand. An empty cup. A marriage. A full house. One night per week in stranger’s white sheets. Forget tomorrow, You say, don’t mention love. I try. It doesn’t work. I assault the postman for a letter. I look for flowers. I go over and over our times together, re-read them. This very second I am waiting on the phone. Silver service. I polish it. I dress for it. I’ll give it extra in return for your call. Infuriatingly, it sends me hoaxes, wrong numbers; or worse, calls from boring people. Your voice disappears into my lonely cotton sheets. I am trapped in it. I can’t move. I want you. All the time. This is awful – only a photo. Come on, damn you, ring me. Or else. What? I don’t know what.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“I am spending my time imagining the worst that could happen.”

Look at how the speaker begins with fear rather than hope.
  • The superlative “worst” establishes anxiety and pessimism immediately.
  • It shows the speaker’s mind is dominated by fear rather than hope.
  • Kay presents love as mentally exhausting before anything has even happened.
QUOTE 2

“The future is a long gloved hand. An empty cup. / A marriage. A full house.”

Think about how the list moves between emptiness and hope.
  • The metaphor “gloved hand” suggests distance, concealment and lack of warmth.
  • The list moves between possibility and emptiness, reflecting uncertainty.
  • The future feels unreachable because the speaker cannot control the relationship.
QUOTE 3

“I am trapped in it. I can’t move. I want you.”

Focus on the short sentences and the feeling of being stuck.
  • The verb “trapped” suggests emotional imprisonment and helplessness.
  • The short sentences create panic and paralysis.
  • Longing has stopped being romantic and has become suffocating.

Dusting the Phone – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Kay present longing in the poem, Dusting the Phone?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on longing and obsession.
  • Introduces the speaker’s emotional dependence on a phone call.
  • Establishes Kay’s message that love can become anxious and suffocating.
In Dusting the Phone, Jackie Kay presents longing as intense, obsessive and emotionally draining. The speaker is completely fixated on waiting for contact from a lover, and this makes ordinary life feel threatening and unstable. Through dramatic imagery, fragmented thoughts and the repeated focus on the phone, Kay suggests that love can quickly shift from something hopeful into something painful and controlling.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that longing begins in the speaker’s anxious imagination.
  • Uses the quotation about imagining the “worst.”
  • Zooms in on the superlative “worst.”
  • Explores the immediate first-person voice.
  • Links to Kay’s interest in intense personal relationships.
At the start of the poem, Kay presents longing as an anxious state of mind that distorts the speaker’s thinking. This is clear in the line “I am spending my time imagining the worst that could happen.” The superlative “worst” shows that the speaker immediately assumes disaster rather than comfort, suggesting love has filled her with fear instead of security. The first-person voice makes the feeling sound personal and immediate, as if we are hearing her thoughts in real time. Kay often writes about relationships and emotional vulnerability, and here she shows how longing can become mentally exhausting. Instead of enjoying love, the speaker is trapped in dread, which reveals how powerful and damaging desire can be.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the uncertainty of the future.
  • Uses the metaphorical list as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the adjective “gloved.”
  • Explores the fractured listing structure.
  • Links to the modern context of unstable relationships and communication.
Kay also presents longing as uncertainty about what lies ahead. This is shown when the speaker says “The future is a long gloved hand. An empty cup. / A marriage. A full house.” The adjective “gloved” suggests distance and lack of warmth, so the future feels covered up and impossible to grasp. The list moves unpredictably from emptiness to intimacy, showing how the speaker swings wildly between hope and disappointment. Structurally, the short sentence fragments reflect disjointed thinking and make the future seem unstable. In a modern poem centred on a phone call, Kay suggests that communication can intensify emotional uncertainty rather than relieve it. The speaker cannot control what will happen, so her longing becomes even more desperate.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that longing becomes a form of imprisonment.
  • Uses the final declaration as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the verb “trapped.”
  • Explores the short sentences and abrupt ending.
  • Shows Kay’s warning that obsession can overpower the self.
By the end of the poem, Kay shows that longing has become almost unbearable. This is captured in the line “I am trapped in it. I can’t move. I want you.” The verb “trapped” suggests imprisonment, as if the speaker is no longer in control of her own emotions. The short sentences create a panicked rhythm and emphasise her helplessness. Even the simple statement “I want you” sounds raw and desperate because it is stripped of romance and reduced to need. The abrupt final question, “What?” followed by “I don’t know what,” leaves the poem unresolved, showing that longing has confused and consumed her. Kay’s final impression is that obsessive love can leave a person emotionally stuck and powerless.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Kay’s presentation of longing.
  • Returns to the idea that love becomes obsession.
  • Highlights the warning about emotional dependence.
In conclusion, Kay presents longing in Dusting the Phone as powerful but deeply unsettling. What begins as waiting for a lover’s call develops into obsession, anxiety and emotional paralysis. Through vivid metaphors, fragmented structure and an intimate voice, Kay suggests that longing can make love feel less comforting than suffocating.
