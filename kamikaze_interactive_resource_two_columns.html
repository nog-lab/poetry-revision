He sets out on a suicide mission but turns back after seeing the sea, fishing boats and memories of childhood.

In Japan and over the surrounding sea. The natural world reminds him of family, home and the value of life.

During World War Two, when kamikaze pilots were expected to die for Japan in suicide attacks.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“enough fuel for a one-way journey into history”

Think about what “one-way” and “history” suggest about duty and death.
  • “One-way” makes death seem certain.
  • “History” suggests honour and national glory.
  • Garland shows how society turns individual death into patriotic symbolism.
QUOTE 2

“strung out like bunting”

Ask why Garland compares fishing boats to something festive and decorative.
  • “Bunting” suggests celebration, colour and ordinary life.
  • The simile contrasts sharply with the violence of the mission.
  • This image becomes a turning point as life and memory pull him back.
QUOTE 3

“to be silent”

Think about silence as punishment, shame and erasure.
  • “Silent” suggests shame and emotional punishment.
  • The family learns society’s rejection and copies it.
  • Garland shows that survival brings isolation rather than peace.

Context

Make notes first, then reveal.

Hint: think about World War Two, kamikaze pilots, honour, shame and family expectations.
  • Kamikaze pilots were Japanese soldiers in World War Two expected to carry out suicide attacks.
  • Dying for the Emperor and the nation was presented as honourable and patriotic.
  • Japanese military culture placed strong value on obedience, loyalty and self-sacrifice.
  • Garland explores the human cost of these expectations when the pilot chooses life.

Structure

Add structure notes, then reveal.

Hint: look at the narrative movement from mission, to memory, to rejection.
  • The poem is a narrative told through the daughter’s reported memory.
  • It shifts from duty and mission to nature, childhood memory and home.
  • The long descriptive middle slows the pace and marks the pilot’s turning point.
  • The ending focuses on silence and rejection, emphasising lasting shame and isolation.

Kamikaze – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Garland present conflict between duty and human instinct in Kamikaze?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on duty, memory and survival.
  • Introduces the conflict between patriotic expectation and human instinct.
  • Establishes Garland’s wider message about honour, shame and isolation.
In Kamikaze, Beatrice Garland presents conflict between duty and human instinct as painful, powerful and impossible to escape. The poem tells the story of a Japanese pilot who sets out on a suicide mission in the Second World War but turns back after being reminded of the beauty of life, nature and family. Through narrative voice, vivid imagery and structural shifts, Garland suggests that the instinct to live is natural and deeply human, yet choosing life brings lasting punishment from society.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the pressure of patriotic duty.
  • Uses the quotation about the “one-way journey into history.”
  • Zooms in on “one-way” and “history.”
  • Explores ritual, honour and expectation.
  • Links to kamikaze missions in WWII Japan.
At the start of the poem, Garland presents duty as something overwhelming and almost sacred. This is shown when the pilot leaves with “enough fuel for a one-way journey into history.” The phrase “one-way” makes his death seem certain, while the noun “history” suggests that he has been taught to believe dying for his country will make him glorious and important. Garland also includes details such as the “samurai sword” and “powerful incantations,” which create a ceremonial tone and show how duty has been wrapped in tradition and honour. Contextually, kamikaze pilots were expected to sacrifice themselves for Japan in a way that was seen as patriotic and noble. Garland therefore presents conflict immediately: the pilot is not only flying towards death, but towards an identity that society has already chosen for him.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the turning point towards life.
  • Uses the image of the boats “strung out like bunting.”
  • Zooms in on “bunting.”
  • Explores natural imagery and memory as forces against war.
  • Links to Garland’s message that life can overpower duty.
Garland then presents human instinct as stronger than patriotic duty when the pilot looks down and sees the fishing boats “strung out like bunting.” The simile transforms the scene into something colourful, festive and alive, and the word “bunting” has connotations of celebration rather than violence. This sharply contrasts with the deadly purpose of his mission and marks a turning point in the poem. The extended natural imagery that follows, including the “green-blue translucent sea” and fish “flashing silver,” slows the pace and draws attention to the beauty of the world he is about to leave. Garland also moves into memories of childhood and family, suggesting that instinct is tied not just to survival but to belonging, love and home. By placing these vivid memories in the centre of the poem, Garland shows that the pilot turns back not because he is weak, but because he remembers what makes life valuable.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the consequences of choosing life.
  • Uses the final phrase “we too learned to be silent.”
  • Zooms in on “silent.”
  • Explores the daughter’s viewpoint and ending.
  • Links to shame and social rejection.
By the end of the poem, Garland presents the aftermath of this conflict as tragic because survival leads to emotional death. This is shown in the final line, where the daughter explains that the family “too learned to be silent.” The adjective “silent” suggests shame, repression and punishment, showing that the father is treated as if his choice to live has made him unworthy of love or recognition. Earlier, the neighbours behave “as though he no longer existed,” which reveals that although he returned physically, he has been erased socially. Structurally, the poem ends not with action but with silence, emphasising the lasting damage caused by society’s expectations. Garland’s use of the daughter’s voice also makes the ending more painful because readers see how this shame spreads through the whole family. In this way, Garland suggests that war destroys more than lives on the battlefield: it also damages identity, relationships and the ability to speak openly.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Garland’s presentation of conflict.
  • Returns to the contrast between duty and the instinct to live.
  • Highlights the warning about honour, shame and sacrifice.
In conclusion, Garland presents conflict in Kamikaze as a struggle between public duty and private humanity. Although the pilot is expected to die for honour, the beauty of nature and the pull of memory persuade him to choose life instead. Through imagery, structure and contextual ideas about shame and patriotism, Garland shows that survival may be natural, but in a society ruled by honour, it can still come at a devastating cost.
