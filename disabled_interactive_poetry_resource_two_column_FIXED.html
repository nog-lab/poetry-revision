5Ws

Write answers on your printed copy first. Reveal each prompt only to check or improve your notes.

Who?

Who is the speaker focusing on, and why is he unnamed?

An unnamed young ex-soldier. Owen leaves him unnamed so he can represent many injured soldiers after World War One.

What?

What has happened to him, and how has his life changed?

He has lost limbs in war and is now isolated, dependent on others, and cut off from his former youth and confidence.

Where?

Where is he now, and where does his memory take him?

He is in a hospital or institution after the war, while his memories return to town life, football, girls and enlistment.

When?

When was the poem written, and what historical context matters?

Written in 1917 during World War One, when propaganda encouraged young men to enlist before understanding the reality of combat.

Why?

Why does Owen contrast the soldier’s past and present?

To expose the physical and emotional damage of war and challenge the false idea that military service is glorious.
DISABLED — Wilfred Owen
He sat in a wheeled chair, waiting for dark, And shivered in his ghastly suit of grey, Legless, sewn short at elbow. Through the park Voices of boys rang saddening like a hymn, Voices of play and pleasure after day, Till gathering sleep had mothered them from him. About this time Town used to swing so gay When glow-lamps budded in the light-blue trees, And girls glanced lovelier as the air grew dim,— In the old times, before he threw away his knees. Now he will never feel again how slim Girls’ waists are, or how warm their subtle hands, All of them touch him like some queer disease. There was an artist silly for his face, For it was younger than his youth, last year. Now, he is old; his back will never brace; He’s lost his colour very far from here, Poured it down shell-holes till the veins ran dry, And half his lifetime lapsed in the hot race, And leap of purple spurted from his thigh.
One time he liked a blood-smear down his leg, After the matches carried shoulder-high. It was after football, when he’d drunk a peg, He thought he’d better join. He wonders why. Someone had said he’d look a god in kilts, That’s why; and maybe, too, to please his Meg, Aye, that was it, to please the giddy jilts He asked to join. He didn’t have to beg; Smiling they wrote his lie: aged nineteen years. Germans he scarcely thought of; and no fears Of Fear came yet. He thought of jewelled hilts For daggers in plaid socks; of smart salutes; And care of arms; and leave; and pay arrears; Esprit de corps; and hints for young recruits. And soon, he was drafted out with drums and cheers. Some cheered him home, but not as crowds cheer Goal. Only a solemn man who brought him fruits Thanked him; and then inquired about his soul. Now, he will spend a few sick years in institutes, And do what things the rules consider wise, And take whatever pity they may dole. Tonight he noticed how the women’s eyes Passed from him to the strong men that were whole. How cold and late it is! Why don’t they come And put him into bed? Why don’t they come?

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“Legless, sewn short at elbow.”

Look at how blunt and incomplete the description feels.
  • “Legless” is blunt and shocking.
  • “Sewn short” makes his body seem damaged and reduced.
  • Owen strips away patriotic glory and shows the real cost of war.
QUOTE 2

“And leap of purple spurted from his thigh.”

Focus on the violence of the verb and colour.
  • “Spurted” creates sudden, graphic violence.
  • “Purple” makes the wound seem grotesque rather than heroic.
  • The image marks the moment his youth and future are destroyed.
QUOTE 3

“Why don’t they come / And put him into bed? Why don’t they come?”

Think about dependence, repetition, and the unanswered question.
  • The repeated “Why” suggests helplessness and desperation.
  • He depends on others for basic care.
  • The unanswered ending emphasises isolation and neglect.

Context

Make notes first, then reveal.

Hint: think about Owen’s war experience, propaganda, and what happened to wounded soldiers after returning home.
  • Wilfred Owen was a First World War soldier and one of the most important anti-war poets.
  • He wrote Disabled in 1917 while recovering from shell shock at Craiglockhart War Hospital.
  • Owen challenged patriotic propaganda that presented war as noble and exciting.
  • The poem reflects the neglect, trauma and isolation many injured soldiers faced after returning home.

Structure

Add structure notes, then reveal.

Hint: look at the movement between past and present, the uneven stanzas, and the repeated ending.
  • Uneven stanzas reflect instability and the soldier’s disrupted life.
  • The poem shifts between past and present to contrast youthful freedom with disabled dependence.
  • The cyclical ending returns him to waiting, reinforcing hopelessness and isolation.
  • The loose rhyme and rhythm create a dragging, unsettled movement.

Disabled – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Owen present the effects of war in Disabled?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on loss and suffering.
  • Introduces the contrast between the soldier’s past youth and present condition.
  • Establishes Owen’s wider anti-war message about the reality behind propaganda.
In Disabled, Wilfred Owen presents the effects of war as devastating, humiliating and permanent. The poem shows a young soldier whose life has been destroyed physically and emotionally after fighting in the First World War. Through shocking imagery, shifts between past and present, and a cyclical structure, Owen exposes the true cost of war and challenges the false idea that military service is glorious or heroic.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the soldier’s immediate physical damage.
  • Uses the blunt description “Legless, sewn short at elbow.”
  • Zooms in on the adjective “Legless.”
  • Explores how the soldier is reduced to his injuries.
  • Links to Owen’s aim to expose the brutality of war.
Owen first presents the effects of war as brutally physical and impossible to ignore. This is clear in the shocking description “Legless, sewn short at elbow.” The adjective “Legless” is blunt and immediate, forcing the reader to confront the severity of the soldier’s injuries. The short, clipped phrasing makes him seem reduced to a damaged body rather than a whole person, showing how war has stripped away his independence and identity. Owen, who wrote the poem in 1917 after experiencing war himself, wanted to challenge patriotic propaganda by revealing what combat really did to young men. Instead of honour and pride, the soldier is left broken and isolated.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment focuses on the moment his life is changed forever.
  • Uses the violent image of the wound as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the verb “spurted.”
  • Explores the contrast with his earlier excitement about war.
  • Links to the poem’s criticism of recruitment and false promises.
Owen also presents war as suddenly destructive, turning youthful excitement into horror. This is shown when he remembers how “And leap of purple spurted from his thigh.” The verb “spurted” creates a vivid, violent image of blood bursting out uncontrollably, making the injury seem immediate and terrifying. The colour “purple” makes the moment sound unnatural and grotesque rather than heroic. This is especially powerful because earlier in the poem the soldier joined up carelessly, thinking about football, girls and how he would look in uniform, while “Germans he scarcely thought of.” Owen exposes how little he understood about war before enlisting, suggesting that young men were encouraged to chase pride and appearance without understanding the terrible reality awaiting them.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the lasting emotional effects of war.
  • Uses the repeated question at the end of the poem.
  • Zooms in on the word “Why.”
  • Explores the cyclical ending and helplessness.
  • Links to the neglect injured soldiers faced after returning home.
By the end of the poem, Owen shows that the effects of war are not only physical but also emotional and lasting. This is clear in the repeated question “Why don’t they come / And put him into bed? Why don’t they come?” The repeated word “Why” suggests desperation and helplessness, as the soldier is now dependent on others for even basic care. The poem’s cyclical structure is important because it returns him to the same lonely, passive position from the opening, showing that his life has become a pattern of waiting and abandonment. Contextually, many wounded soldiers returned from the First World War to lives of pity and neglect rather than celebration. Owen therefore suggests that war does not end when the fighting stops, because its trauma continues long after the battlefield has been left behind.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Owen’s presentation of war’s effects.
  • Returns to the contrast between youth and ruin.
  • Highlights the poem’s warning about propaganda and sacrifice.
In conclusion, Owen presents the effects of war in Disabled as cruel, life-changing and deeply tragic. The soldier loses his body, his confidence and his future, and is left isolated with only memories of the life he once had. Through shocking language, structural contrasts and clear anti-war purpose, Owen warns readers that war destroys far more than it ever gives.
