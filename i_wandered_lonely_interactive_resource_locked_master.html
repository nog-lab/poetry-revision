I WANDERED LONELY AS A CLOUD — William Wordsworth
I wandered lonely as a cloud That floats on high o'er vales and hills, When all at once I saw a crowd, A host, of golden daffodils; Beside the lake, beneath the trees, Fluttering and dancing in the breeze. Continuous as the stars that shine And twinkle on the milky way, They stretched in never-ending line Along the margin of a bay: Ten thousand saw I at a glance, Tossing their heads in sprightly dance. The waves beside them danced; but they Out-did the sparkling waves in glee: A poet could not but be gay, In such a jocund company: I gazed—and gazed—but little thought What wealth the show to me had brought: For oft, when on my couch I lie In vacant or in pensive mood, They flash upon that inward eye Which is the bliss of solitude; And then my heart with pleasure fills, And dances with the daffodils.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“I wandered lonely as a cloud / That floats on high o’er vales and hills”

Think about the opening mood and the simile.
  • “Lonely” establishes isolation and melancholy at the start of the poem.
  • The simile “as a cloud” suggests he is drifting alone above the landscape, emotionally separate from others.
  • This opening makes the later joy of the daffodils feel more powerful because nature transforms his mood.
QUOTE 2

“A poet could not but be gay, / In such a jocund company”

Think about personification and the idea of companionship.
  • “Jocund” means cheerful and light-hearted, showing the speaker’s uplifted mood.
  • The phrase “jocund company” personifies the daffodils as companions, so nature feels social and welcoming.
  • Wordsworth presents nature as a source of emotional connection, not just something beautiful to look at.
QUOTE 3

“And then my heart with pleasure fills, / And dances with the daffodils”

Think about memory and the poem’s final emotional change.
  • “Fills” suggests lasting happiness, as the memory of the daffodils completely restores his mood.
  • The personification of his heart that “dances” shows how deeply nature’s joy stays with him.
  • This ending reflects a Romantic idea: memory and imagination allow nature to comfort the speaker even when he is alone.

I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Wordsworth present the power of nature in I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on nature’s emotional power.
  • Introduces the movement from loneliness to joy.
  • Establishes Wordsworth’s message about memory and the lasting effect of nature.
In I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud, William Wordsworth presents nature as joyful, uplifting and powerful enough to transform human emotion. The speaker begins the poem feeling isolated, but the sight of the daffodils changes his mood and leaves him with a memory that continues to comfort him later. Through personification, imagery and regular structure, Wordsworth shows that nature is not only beautiful in the moment but also spiritually restorative long afterwards.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the speaker’s isolation at the start.
  • Uses the opening simile as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the adjective “lonely.”
  • Explores how the opening prepares for nature’s effect.
  • Links to Romantic ideas about individual feeling.
At the start of the poem, Wordsworth presents nature as powerful because it interrupts the speaker’s loneliness and emotional detachment. This is clear in the line “I wandered lonely as a cloud.” The adjective “lonely” immediately establishes a sense of isolation, while the simile compares the speaker to a drifting cloud, suggesting that he feels aimless and separate from the world below. This makes the appearance of the daffodils more significant, because nature becomes the force that restores his happiness. As a Romantic poet, Wordsworth often focused on personal emotion and individual experience, and here he suggests that nature can heal the mind when a person feels cut off or empty.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the joy and companionship nature gives.
  • Uses the phrase about a “jocund company.”
  • Zooms in on the adjective “jocund.”
  • Explores personification of the daffodils.
  • Links to Wordsworth’s belief in nature’s emotional value.
Wordsworth also presents nature as joyful and almost human in the comfort it offers. This is shown when the speaker says “In such a jocund company.” The adjective “jocund” means cheerful and light-hearted, showing that the daffodils seem full of energy and happiness. By describing them as a “company,” Wordsworth personifies the flowers so they seem like companions rather than simply part of the landscape. This suggests that nature can replace loneliness with connection and warmth. The regular rhyme and smooth rhythm also reflect harmony, reinforcing the idea that the natural world offers balance and emotional relief. In this way, Wordsworth presents nature as a source of joy that deeply affects the speaker’s state of mind.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the lasting power of memory.
  • Uses the final line as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the verb “fills.”
  • Explores the shift from experience to reflection.
  • Links to the Romantic value of imagination and memory.
By the end of the poem, Wordsworth shows that nature remains powerful even when it is no longer physically present. This is captured in the line “And then my heart with pleasure fills, / And dances with the daffodils.” The verb “fills” suggests that the memory of the flowers completely restores the speaker’s happiness. The personification of his heart that “dances” shows how strongly nature continues to live within him. Structurally, the final stanza shifts from the original moment in nature to later reflection in solitude, showing that the true value of the experience is its lasting effect. This reflects the Romantic idea that memory and imagination allow nature to continue nourishing the mind long after the scene itself has passed.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Wordsworth’s presentation of nature.
  • Returns to the contrast between loneliness and joy.
  • Highlights the lasting emotional power of memory.
In conclusion, Wordsworth presents nature as a powerful force that can lift the human spirit and leave a lasting emotional impression. The daffodils change the speaker from lonely to joyful, and their memory continues to comfort him afterwards. Through vivid imagery, personification and reflective structure, Wordsworth suggests that nature offers both immediate happiness and enduring inner peace.
