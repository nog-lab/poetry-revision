Who?

Who is the speaker, and whose story is being told?

The speaker is Eve L Ewing, reflecting on her mother and father and the relationship that forms part of her own family identity.

What?

What does the poem describe?

It tells the story of how her parents met, then compares their love to a fragile comic book that was valued but not carefully preserved.

Where?

Where is the poem rooted?

It is rooted in Chicago, with everyday references such as the Greyhound bus station, music, comics and young Black American life.

When?

When do the events and publication context matter?

Her parents meet in the mid-1980s; the poem was published in Electric Arches in 2017.

Why?

Why does Ewing tell this story?

To celebrate family history, identity and love, while showing that a relationship can still matter deeply even if it does not last.
ORIGIN STORY — Eve L Ewing
This is true: my mother and my father met at the Greyhound bus station in the mid-eighties in Chicago. my mother, all thick glass and afro puff, came west on the train when she was nineteen, lived in a friend’s house and cared for her children, played tambourine in a Chaka Khan cover band. my father, all sleeveless and soft eye, ran away from home when he was seventeen, mimeographed communist newspapers and drew comic books like this one, for sale. one dollar. my mother bought one. love is like a comic book. it’s fragile and the best we can do is protect it in whatever clumsy ways we can: plastic and cardboard, dark rooms and boxes. in this way, something never meant to last might find its way to another decade, another home, an attic, a basement, intact. love is paper. and if my parents’ love was a comic book, it never saw polyvinyl, never felt a backing. it was curled into a back pocket for a day at the park, lent to a friend, read under covers, reread hanging upside-down over the back of the couch, memorized, mishandled, worn thin, staples rusted. a love like that doesn’t last but it has a good ending.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“This is true: my mother and my father met at the Greyhound bus station”

Think about why the poem begins by insisting the story is true.
  • “This is true” creates a confident, declarative opening.
  • The phrase suggests Ewing is claiming ownership of her family story.
  • “Greyhound bus station” roots love in ordinary, realistic detail.
QUOTE 2

“love is like a comic book. it’s fragile”

Focus on the simile and the word “fragile”.
  • The simile makes love feel vivid, personal and culturally specific.
  • “Fragile” suggests love can be easily damaged.
  • The comic-book image links love to memory, value and preservation.
QUOTE 3

“a love like that doesn’t last but it has a good ending.”

Look at the contrast between ending and value.
  • “Doesn’t last” is honest about the relationship ending.
  • “Good ending” creates warmth rather than bitterness.
  • The ending suggests love can still matter through memory and family.

Origin Story – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Ewing present love in the poem, Origin Story?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on love as precious but fragile.
  • Introduces the poem’s blend of family memory and personal reflection.
  • Establishes Ewing’s wider message that love may not last forever but can still matter deeply.
In Origin Story, Eve L Ewing presents love as personal, fragile and valuable because of the life and memories it creates. The poem begins by telling the speaker’s parents’ story in a vivid, affectionate way before reflecting on how their relationship should be understood. Through a conversational voice, extended comic-book metaphor and thoughtful structure, Ewing suggests that love does not need to be permanent to be meaningful.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that love is presented as real and rooted in family history.
  • Uses the opening quotation “This is true:”
  • Zooms in on the declarative phrase “true.”
  • Explores Ewing’s first-person voice and storytelling style.
  • Links to the poem’s autobiographical and identity-based context.
At the start of the poem, Ewing presents love as something real and worth preserving through memory. This is clear in the opening statement “This is true:” The word “true” gives the poem an immediate sense of certainty, as if the speaker is carefully recording an important family history. Ewing then tells the story of how her parents met in an ordinary place, making their love feel authentic rather than idealised. The first-person perspective and free verse form create a natural, intimate voice, as though the speaker is passing down a personal story. This matters because the poem is rooted in Ewing’s own background and reflects pride in family identity, showing that love becomes part of the speaker’s understanding of who she is.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies love as delicate and easily damaged.
  • Uses the central metaphor as evidence.
  • Zooms in on the adjective “fragile.”
  • Explores the extended comic-book metaphor and modern imagery.
  • Links to Ewing’s interest in comics and contemporary culture.
Ewing also presents love as delicate and difficult to protect. This is shown in the line “love is like a comic book. it’s fragile” The adjective “fragile” suggests that love can easily be bent, torn or damaged if it is not looked after. By comparing love to a comic book, Ewing uses a modern extended metaphor that makes the relationship feel both valuable and vulnerable. The image is effective because comic books are often loved intensely, yet they can be treated carelessly and wear out over time. Structurally, the poem shifts here from narrative into reflection, moving from the parents’ meeting to the speaker’s interpretation of what their love meant. This also connects to Ewing’s wider work with comic books, making the metaphor feel personal and purposeful rather than random.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains that love is still valuable even when it ends.
  • Uses the final quotation about the “good ending.”
  • Zooms in on the contrast between “doesn’t last” and “good.”
  • Explores the poem’s ending and its tone of acceptance.
  • Links to ideas of family, legacy and the speaker’s existence.
By the end of the poem, Ewing suggests that love can still be successful even if it does not last forever. This is captured in the final lines “a love like that doesn’t last / but it has a good ending.” The contrast between “doesn’t last” and “good” is important because it challenges the idea that only permanent love has value. Instead, Ewing implies that the relationship mattered because it created joy, memory and, ultimately, the speaker herself. The short closing statement gives the ending a calm, confident tone, and the simplicity of the line makes it feel truthful. Rather than sounding bitter, the poem finishes with acceptance and gratitude, suggesting that love should be judged by what it gives, not only by how long it survives.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Ewing’s presentation of love.
  • Returns to the contrast between fragility and lasting meaning.
  • Highlights the poem’s message about memory, family and value.
In conclusion, Ewing presents love in Origin Story as fragile but deeply significant. Although the relationship she describes was not permanent, it shaped identity, family and memory in lasting ways. Through personal voice, extended metaphor and reflective structure, the poem shows that love can still have a “good ending” when it leaves behind meaning, gratitude and a story worth preserving.
