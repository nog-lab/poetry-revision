Sonnet 29

5Ws

Write answers on your printed copy first. Reveal each prompt only to check or improve your notes.

Who?

Who is the speaker addressing, and what is her emotional state?

The speaker is Elizabeth Barrett Browning addressing her lover, Robert Browning. She feels intense longing and desire for his real presence.

What?

What does the speaker want instead of just thinking about her lover?

She wants his real, physical presence. Her thoughts are powerful, but they are not enough to satisfy her longing.

Where?

Where does the poem seem to take place?

Mostly in the speaker’s inner world. She uses natural imagery of vines and trees to picture her thoughts and her lover.

When?

When was the poem written, and what relationship does it reflect?

It was written during Barrett Browning’s relationship with Robert Browning and published in Sonnets from the Portuguese in 1850.

Why?

Why might Browning present longing through natural imagery?

The vine and tree imagery makes longing seem alive, growing and uncontrollable, while also showing her desire for real closeness.
SONNET 29 — Elizabeth Barrett Browning
I think of thee!—my thoughts do twine and bud About thee, as wild vines, about a tree, Put out broad leaves, and soon there’s nought to see Except the straggling green which hides the wood. Yet, O my palm-tree, be it understood I will not have my thoughts instead of thee Who art dearer, better! Rather, instantly Renew thy presence; as a strong tree should, Rustle thy boughs and set thy trunk all bare, And let these bands of greenery which insphere thee Drop heavily down,—burst, shattered, everywhere! Because, in this deep joy to see and hear thee And breathe within thy shadow a new air, I do not think of thee—I am too near thee.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“my thoughts do twine and bud”

Focus on how the verbs make her thoughts seem alive and growing.
  • “Twine” suggests wrapping and clinging.
  • “Bud” suggests growth and new life.
  • The extended metaphor presents longing as natural, powerful and hard to control.
QUOTE 2

“Rustle thy boughs and set thy trunk all bare”

Think about the imperative verbs and the shift from thought to physical presence.
  • “Rustle” and “set” are commands, showing urgency.
  • “Trunk” symbolises the lover’s real physical presence.
  • “Bare” suggests openness, exposure and the removal of imagined thoughts.
QUOTE 3

“And breathe within thy shadow a new air”

Look at how closeness is made physical and renewing.
  • “Breathe” makes love feel physical and immediate.
  • “Shadow” suggests closeness, protection and surrounding presence.
  • “New air” implies renewal: his presence transforms her world.

Context

Make notes first, then reveal.

Hint: think about Browning’s relationship, Victorian expectations, and why a female speaker expressing desire matters.
  • Elizabeth Barrett Browning was a successful Victorian poet who experienced illness and isolation.
  • Her father was controlling and strongly disapproved of his children marrying.
  • She secretly courted and later eloped with fellow poet Robert Browning.
  • Sonnets from the Portuguese is a private love-poem sequence about their relationship.

Structure

  • Petrarchan sonnet: a traditional form used to express love and intense emotion.

  • The volta marks a shift from passive longing to urgent command.
  • The extended vine/tree metaphor structures the poem’s argument about thought, desire and closeness.

Sonnet 29 – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Browning present longing in the poem, Sonnet 29?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on love, desire and longing.
  • Introduces the contrast between imagined love and real presence.
  • Establishes Browning’s wider message that true closeness is more powerful than fantasy.
In Sonnet 29, Elizabeth Barrett Browning presents longing as intense, consuming and deeply physical. At the start of the poem, the speaker is overwhelmed by thoughts of her lover, but as the sonnet develops, she makes it clear that imagination is not enough. Through natural imagery, imperatives and the structure of the sonnet, Browning shows that longing is painful because thought can never fully replace real human closeness.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how longing first feels overwhelming and uncontrollable.
  • Uses the quotation about thoughts that “twine and bud.”
  • Zooms in on the verbs “twine” and “bud.”
  • Explores the extended metaphor of vines and a tree.
  • Links to Romantic interest in emotion and nature.
Browning first presents longing as something that grows powerfully inside the speaker’s mind. This is shown when she says “my thoughts do twine and bud.” The verb “twine” suggests her thoughts are wrapping themselves around her lover, showing how completely he fills her mind. Meanwhile, “bud” suggests growth, making her love seem alive and still developing. The extended metaphor of the wild vine and the tree shows that her desire is natural and passionate, but also so strong that it begins to take over everything else. This reflects Romantic ideas about emotion being powerful and untamed, as Browning presents longing as a force that cannot easily be controlled.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies the speaker’s impatience for real closeness.
  • Uses the command about setting the “trunk all bare.”
  • Zooms in on the imperative “set” and adjective “bare.”
  • Explores the volta and the shift from thought to action.
  • Links to Victorian expectations of female restraint.
Browning also presents longing as urgent because the speaker no longer wants thoughts alone and instead demands real presence. This is clear in the line “Rustle thy boughs and set thy trunk all bare.” The imperative “set” shows how strongly she desires action, while “bare” suggests openness, exposure and physical reality. At this point in the sonnet, the speaker moves away from passive imagining and towards a direct command, which marks the poem’s volta. This turning point is important because it shows that longing has become too intense to remain private. In a Victorian context, this is striking, as Browning gives a female speaker a bold, passionate voice that challenges the idea women should be quiet or restrained about desire.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how longing is finally resolved by closeness.
  • Uses the quotation about breathing within his “shadow a new air.”
  • Zooms in on “breathe” and “new air.”
  • Explores sensory language and the ending of the sonnet.
  • Links to ideas of renewal and emotional freedom.
By the end of the poem, Browning suggests that longing can only be satisfied through real nearness. This is expressed in “And breathe within thy shadow a new air.” The verb “breathe” makes the feeling intimate and physical, suggesting that love is no longer just a thought but something fully experienced. The phrase “new air” implies renewal, as though being close to her lover transforms her whole world. The sensory language here contrasts with the earlier focus on thinking, showing that physical presence is more powerful than imagination. The final line, where she says she does not think of him because she is “too near,” powerfully ends the poem by showing that longing disappears only when love becomes real.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Browning’s presentation of longing.
  • Returns to the contrast between thought and presence.
  • Highlights the poem’s message that love is strongest when fully experienced.
In conclusion, Browning presents longing in Sonnet 29 as passionate, restless and ultimately unsatisfied by imagination alone. Although the speaker’s thoughts reveal the strength of her love, the poem makes clear that real presence matters more than fantasy. Through natural imagery, sonnet form and a confident female voice, Browning shows that longing is only resolved when emotional desire becomes physical closeness.
