CATRIN — Gillian Clarke
I can remember you, child, As I stood in a hot, white Room at the window watching The people and cars taking Turn at the traffic lights. I can remember you, our first fierce confrontation, the tight red rope of love which we both Fought over. It was a square Environmental blank, disinfected Of paintings or toys. I wrote All over the walls with my Words, coloured the clean squares With the wild, tender circles Of our struggle to become Separate. We want, we shouted, To be two, to be ourselves. Neither won nor lost the struggle In the glass tank clouded with feelings Which changed us both. Still I am fighting you off, as you stand there With your straight, strong, long Brown hair and your rosy, Defiant glare, bringing up From the heart’s pool that old rope, Tightening about my life, Trailing love and conflict, As you ask may you skate In the dark, for one more hour.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

QUOTE 1

“our first / fierce confrontation”

Think about why childbirth is described like a battle.
  • “Fierce” suggests intensity, pain and emotional force.
  • “Confrontation” makes birth sound like conflict.
  • Clarke challenges idealised ideas of motherhood by showing love can begin in struggle.
QUOTE 2

“the tight / red rope of love”

Ask what the rope connects, and what it restricts.
  • The metaphor suggests connection, dependence and pain at once.
  • “Red” links to blood, birth, passion and danger.
  • Love binds mother and daughter together even as they try to separate.
QUOTE 3

“Still I am / fighting you off”

Look at what “Still” suggests about time.
  • “Still” shows the struggle has continued from birth into adolescence.
  • “Fighting” makes the emotional tension sound physical.
  • Clarke shows that family love can be lasting but also full of conflict.

Catrin – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Clarke present conflict in the poem, Catrin?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on love and conflict together.
  • Introduces the contrast between motherly love and the struggle for independence.
  • Establishes Clarke’s message that close relationships can be both tender and difficult.
In Catrin, Gillian Clarke presents conflict as a natural part of the relationship between mother and daughter. The poem shows that, from birth onwards, their bond is shaped by both intense love and a constant struggle for independence. Through vivid metaphors, forceful language and a clear shift from past to present, Clarke suggests that family conflict is painful but unavoidable because growing up means becoming separate.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how conflict begins at birth.
  • Uses the quotation “our first fierce confrontation.”
  • Zooms in on the adjective “fierce” and the noun “confrontation.”
  • Explores childbirth as both loving and violent.
  • Links to Clarke’s autobiographical perspective and female experience.
Clarke first presents conflict as something that exists from the very beginning of the mother-daughter relationship. This is shown in the phrase “our first fierce confrontation”. The adjective “fierce” suggests intensity, pain and emotional force, while the noun “confrontation” makes childbirth sound like a struggle rather than a calm, sentimental moment. This immediately challenges idealised ideas of motherhood by showing that love can begin in difficulty. As an autobiographical poem, Catrin draws on Clarke’s own experiences as a mother, which makes the conflict feel personal and truthful. The poem therefore presents conflict not as a sign of failed love, but as part of the powerful physical and emotional experience of bringing a child into the world.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment identifies how love itself creates tension.
  • Uses the metaphor “the tight red rope of love.”
  • Zooms in on “tight” and “red.”
  • Explores connection, pain and dependence together.
  • Links to the poem’s exploration of family relationships and separation.
Clarke also presents conflict as inseparable from love itself. This is clear in the metaphor “the tight / red rope of love”, which suggests that the mother and daughter are bound together in a relationship that is both strong and restrictive. The adjective “tight” implies pressure and strain, showing that their connection is not always comfortable, while “red” links to blood, birth and passion, reminding the reader that this bond is both biological and emotional. Clarke often explores family and identity in her poetry, and here she shows that the very thing which joins mother and daughter together is also what creates tension between them. The conflict is powerful because both characters want different things: closeness on one hand and independence on the other.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how the struggle continues over time.
  • Uses the quotation “Still I am / fighting you off.”
  • Zooms in on the adverb “Still” and the verb “fighting.”
  • Explores the shift from birth to teenage rebellion.
  • Links to the two-stanza structure and ongoing tension.
By the second stanza, Clarke shows that this conflict has not ended but has simply changed form. The line “Still I am / fighting you off” makes it clear that the struggle from childbirth continues into the daughter’s adolescence. The adverb “Still” emphasises duration, suggesting that conflict has become a permanent part of their relationship, while the verb “fighting” is forceful and physical, showing how hard the mother finds it to let go. Structurally, the poem’s movement from the past memory of birth to the present moment of asking “for one more hour” shows that the same emotional battle is repeated over time. Clarke presents conflict as ongoing because a daughter’s growing independence can feel, to a mother, like both a success and a loss.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Clarke’s presentation of conflict.
  • Returns to the relationship between love and separation.
  • Highlights the poem’s message that conflict can be part of deep affection.
In conclusion, Clarke presents conflict in Catrin as a lasting and unavoidable feature of the mother-daughter relationship. From the violence of birth to the tensions of adolescence, the poem shows that love does not remove conflict but can actually create it. Through powerful imagery, autobiographical context and a structure that traces the struggle over time, Clarke suggests that growing up means learning to love while also letting go.
