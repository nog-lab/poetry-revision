War Photographer

5Ws

Write answers on your printed copy first. Reveal each prompt only to check or improve your notes.

Who?

Who is the speaker focused on, and what is his role?

A third-person speaker describes a war photographer who develops images after returning home from conflict zones.

What?

What does the poem explore?

The poem explores war, trauma, guilt, human suffering and the public’s brief, shallow response to images of conflict.

Where?

Where is the poem set, and which places are remembered?

It is set in a darkroom in rural England, but the photographer remembers Belfast, Beirut and Phnom Penh.

When?

When was the poem published, and what kind of conflict does it reflect?

Published in 1985, the poem reflects late twentieth-century conflict and the way war was reported through photography and newspapers.

Why?

Why might Duffy want readers to think about war photography?

Duffy shows the horror of war, the burden carried by those who witness it, and society’s desensitisation to suffering.
WAR PHOTOGRAPHER — Carol Ann Duffy
In his dark room he is finally alone with spools of suffering set out in ordered rows. The only light is red and softly glows, as though this were a church and he a priest preparing to intone a Mass. Belfast. Beirut. Phnom Penh. All flesh is grass. He has a job to do. Solutions slop in trays beneath his hands, which did not tremble then though seem to now. Rural England. Home again to ordinary pain which simple weather can dispel, to fields which don’t explode beneath the feet of running children in a nightmare heat. Something is happening. A stranger’s features faintly start to twist before his eyes, a half-formed ghost. He remembers the cries of this man’s wife, how he sought approval without words to do what someone must and how the blood stained into foreign dust. A hundred agonies in black and white from which his editor will pick out five or six for Sunday’s supplement. The reader’s eyeballs prick with tears between the bath and pre-lunch beers. From the aeroplane he stares impassively at where he earns his living and they do not care.

Three key quotes

Find the quotes on paper first. Then reveal highlights, hints, and annotations.

ORDERED

“with spools of suffering set out in ordered rows.”

Think about the contrast between order and suffering.
  • “Spools” reminds us the suffering has been turned into photographs.
  • “Suffering” shows the images contain real pain and death.
  • “Ordered” contrasts with the chaos of war, suggesting the photographer tries to control what he has witnessed.
NIGHTMARE

“of running children in a nightmare heat.”

Focus on how children and heat make war seem terrifying.
  • “Children” stresses innocence and vulnerability.
  • “Nightmare” suggests terror, trauma and something impossible to escape.
  • Duffy contrasts this horror with safe “Rural England”, making the war zones seem even more disturbing.
IMPASSIVELY

“From the aeroplane he stares impassively”

Ask what emotional numbness suggests about repeated exposure to war.
  • “Impassively” suggests emotional numbness and detachment.
  • The photographer has seen so much suffering that he has to suppress his response.
  • Duffy also criticises the readers who briefly feel pity but ultimately “do not care.”

  • The poem refers to real places affected by conflict: Belfast, Beirut and Phnom Penh.
  • Published in 1985, it reflects how war was often consumed through newspapers and media images.
  • Duffy questions whether people at home truly care about distant suffering.

War Photographer – Question 1 Model

Question: How does Duffy present the effects of war in the poem, War Photographer?

Introduction

  • Directly answers the question by focusing on trauma, suffering and indifference.
  • Introduces the contrast between the war zones and the safety of home.
  • Establishes Duffy’s wider message about the emotional cost of conflict.
In War Photographer, Carol Ann Duffy presents the effects of war as shocking, lasting and emotionally damaging. The poem shows not only the suffering of innocent victims in conflict zones, but also the trauma carried by the photographer who records these horrors. Through vivid imagery, controlled structure and strong contrasts, Duffy suggests that war leaves deep scars while people at home often respond only briefly before returning to ordinary life.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains how war is turned into something the photographer tries to control.
  • Uses the quotation about “ordered rows.”
  • Zooms in on the adjective “ordered.”
  • Explores the contrast between neat arrangement and violent suffering.
  • Links to the reality of war photographers recording real conflict.
At the start of the poem, Duffy presents war as so horrific that the photographer must try to impose order on it. This is shown in the phrase “spools of suffering set out in ordered rows.” The noun “suffering” immediately reminds the reader that the photographs contain real pain and death, while the adjective “ordered” suggests the photographer is trying to organise chaos into something manageable. This contrast highlights one of war’s effects: it forces people to control their emotions in order to cope. Duffy was influenced by real war photographers such as Don McCullin, and the line reflects how these individuals must record terrible events without being able to stop them. The regular stanza structure also mirrors this idea of control, making the horrors of war feel even more disturbing because they are contained so calmly.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment focuses on the suffering of innocent people in war.
  • Uses the image of “running children.”
  • Zooms in on the word “nightmare.”
  • Explores contrast between Rural England and conflict zones.
  • Links to the poem’s references to real places affected by war.
Duffy also presents war as something that destroys innocence and turns ordinary life into terror. This is clear in the image of the “running children in a nightmare heat.” The word “children” is important because it emphasises that war harms the most vulnerable, while the noun “nightmare” suggests something frightening, unreal and impossible to escape. Duffy contrasts this with “Rural England” and its “ordinary pain,” showing how distant and protected British life seems compared with the fear experienced in places such as Belfast, Beirut and Phnom Penh. By listing these real conflict zones, Duffy reminds the reader that the suffering is not fictional. The effect of war is therefore both physical and psychological: children must flee for their lives, and the photographer is left haunted by the scenes he has witnessed.

Comment – Evidence – Development

  • Comment explains the emotional numbness caused by repeated exposure to war.
  • Uses the adverb “impassively.”
  • Zooms in on how detached the response becomes.
  • Explores the poem’s critical ending about public indifference.
  • Links to Duffy’s message about media consumption of suffering.
By the end of the poem, Duffy presents one of war’s most disturbing effects as emotional detachment. As the photographer leaves, “he stares impassively” at the places where he works. The adverb “impassively” suggests numbness and repression, implying that he has seen so much suffering that he can no longer respond openly. This makes him a tragic figure, as war has damaged his ability to feel naturally. However, Duffy’s criticism extends beyond him to society itself. The readers’ eyes only “prick / with tears” briefly before they move on, and the final statement “they do not care” is blunt and accusatory. Duffy suggests that war is not only destructive on the battlefield; it also creates indifference in those who consume images of suffering from a distance, reducing real agony to something temporary and forgettable.

Conclusion

  • Summarises Duffy’s presentation of the effects of war.
  • Returns to the contrast between real suffering and distant spectators.
  • Highlights the poem’s warning about trauma and indifference.
In conclusion, Duffy presents the effects of war as brutal and long-lasting. War harms innocent people directly, but it also traumatises those who witness it and exposes the shallow responses of people safely removed from conflict. Through contrast, imagery and carefully controlled structure, Duffy shows that war leaves suffering behind long after the photograph has been taken.
